# Product Photography

Sub-skill for e-commerce product shots, lifestyle product images, packaging mockups, and any image where a physical product is the subject.

---

## Understanding Phase

Before writing a prompt, extract these from the user's context:

1. **Product attributes.** What is the product? What is it made of (glass, metal, leather, fabric, wood, plastic)? What color? What size relative to common objects? What are the key visual features — texture, finish, hardware, labels, transparency?
2. **Brand aesthetic.** Is this brand premium, playful, minimal, rugged, clinical, luxurious, handmade? The lighting, background, and styling must express the brand even before the viewer reads a logo.
3. **Intended platform.** E-commerce listing (Amazon, Shopify), social media (Instagram, Pinterest), print catalog, advertising, packaging render? Each platform has different requirements for aspect ratio, background, and composition.
4. **Target audience.** Who is buying this? A skincare product for 25-year-old women requires different photography than the same product marketed to dermatologists.
5. **Shot type needed.** Hero shot (dramatic, aspirational), standard listing shot (clean, informational), detail/macro shot (texture, craftsmanship), lifestyle shot (in-context use), group/collection shot (multiple SKUs together), scale shot (showing size relative to hand or common object).

### Two Fundamental Modes

**Studio / Clean:** White or neutral background. Controlled lighting. Product is isolated. Purpose is informational — show the product clearly, accurately, completely. This is the e-commerce standard. Amazon requires pure white backgrounds. Shopify merchants use it. It's clinical by design.

**Lifestyle / Contextual:** Product is placed in an environment. Natural or styled lighting. Props, surfaces, and settings tell a story. Purpose is aspirational — help the viewer imagine owning and using this product. This is the social media, advertising, and editorial standard.

Most products need both. Understand which one the user needs before prompting.

### Material Matters

The product's material is the single most important factor in choosing lighting. Glass, metal, fabric, and matte plastic all behave completely differently under light. Get the material wrong and the entire image fails, no matter how good the composition is.

## Studio Product Photography Principles

### 1. Lighting
Lighting is the single most important element in product photography. Every other decision is secondary.

**Light direction:**
- **Front light:** Flat, even, minimal shadow. Safe but boring. Use for clinical documentation.
- **Side light (45°):** Reveals texture and form. The workhorse of product photography.
- **Back light:** Creates rim glow, reveals translucency. Essential for glass and liquids.
- **Top light:** Natural feel, simulates overhead daylight. Good for flat-lay and food.
- **Under light:** Dramatic, unusual. Use sparingly for special effect (tech products, luxury).

**Light quality:**
- **Soft / diffused:** Large source relative to subject. Wrapping, gentle gradients, subtle shadows. Use for most products, especially skin-contact items (cosmetics, clothing).
- **Hard / directional:** Small source relative to subject. Sharp shadows, defined edges, punchy contrast. Use to emphasize texture (leather grain, fabric weave, metal brushing).

**Number of sources:**
- **One key light:** Simple, dramatic. Strong shadows on one side. Good for moody, editorial product shots.
- **Key + fill:** Key light from one side, fill light (softer, weaker) from the other to open up shadows. The standard two-light product setup.
- **Key + fill + rim/accent:** Adds a backlight or edge light to separate the product from the background. Use for dark products on dark backgrounds, or for any product where edge definition matters.

### 2. Background
- **Pure white (RGB 255,255,255):** E-commerce standard. Amazon requires it. Specify explicitly — AI models may generate off-white or gray if you just say "white background."
- **Gradient gray:** Smooth gradient from light to dark. More depth than flat white. Good for premium products, electronics, tools.
- **Colored background:** Match to brand palette or create complementary contrast. A warm product pops on a cool background and vice versa.
- **Textured surface:** Marble, wood, concrete, fabric, slate. Adds material context without a full lifestyle scene. The surface material implies a brand world.
- **Black / dark:** Dramatic, premium. Requires careful rim lighting to prevent the product from disappearing into the background.

### 3. Composition
- **Centered:** Product dead-center. Symmetrical. The e-commerce default. Boring but functional.
- **Rule of thirds:** Product placed at a third-line intersection. More dynamic, more editorial. Better for hero shots and social media.
- **Camera angle matters enormously:**
  - **Eye level (0°):** Natural, relatable. How you'd see the product on a table.
  - **Slight elevation (15–30°):** The most common product angle. Shows top and front simultaneously.
  - **45° above:** Shows the product top clearly. Good for packaging, open boxes, food.
  - **Top-down / flat-lay (90°):** Looking straight down. Good for collections, small items, food, cosmetics.
  - **Hero angle (low, looking up):** Makes the product feel powerful, imposing. Good for bottles, electronics, shoes.

### 4. Shadow
Shadows ground a product in physical space. Without them, the product floats.
- **No shadow:** Required for some e-commerce platforms (pure white cutout). Product looks like a catalog entry.
- **Contact shadow:** A subtle, tight shadow directly beneath the product. Grounds it without drama. The versatile default.
- **Natural cast shadow:** A visible, directional shadow that implies a specific light source. Adds depth and atmosphere.
- **Reflection:** Product sits on a glossy/reflective surface. Premium, dramatic. Common for electronics and luxury goods.

### 5. Product Scale in Frame
- **E-commerce listing:** Product fills 80–85% of the frame. Tight crop, maximum detail.
- **Hero / editorial:** Product fills 40–60% of the frame. More breathing room, more atmosphere.
- **Detail / macro:** Tight crop on a specific feature — stitching, texture, logo, hardware. Product may bleed off frame edges.
- **Collection / group:** Multiple products arranged together. Each product is smaller but the arrangement itself is the composition.

## Lifestyle Product Photography Principles

### 1. Environment
The setting tells the brand story. The same coffee mug photographed on a marble kitchen counter, a reclaimed-wood workshop bench, or a glass office desk implies three completely different brands and price points. Be specific about the environment — not just "kitchen" but "bright Scandinavian kitchen with white oak countertop and morning light through a large window."

### 2. Context of Use
Show the product being used, about to be used, or just after use. A watch on a wrist. A backpack on a hiker's shoulder. A candle burning on a nightstand. This helps the viewer project themselves into ownership. The product should be the clear subject, but the activity gives it meaning.

### 3. Props and Styling
Supporting elements reinforce the brand narrative without stealing attention. A premium notebook photographed next to a luxury pen and an espresso cup. A running shoe next to a water bottle and earbuds on a gym bench. Props should feel intentional, never random. Every element in frame must earn its place.

### 4. Natural Light
Lifestyle photography thrives on natural or natural-looking light.
- **Golden hour (early morning / late afternoon):** Warm, directional, long shadows. Aspirational, emotional.
- **Morning light:** Cool, clean, soft. Fresh and energetic.
- **Overcast / diffused:** Even, shadowless, soft. Approachable, calm.
- **Dappled light:** Light filtered through leaves or blinds. Organic, textured, photographic.
- Always specify the direction: "warm light entering from the left through a window" not just "natural light."

### 5. Depth of Field
- **Shallow DOF (f/1.4–f/2.8):** Product sharp, background blurred. Isolates the product within the scene. Editorial, intimate.
- **Moderate DOF (f/4–f/8):** Product and immediate surroundings sharp, distant background soft. The versatile default for lifestyle.
- **Deep DOF (f/11+):** Everything sharp. Environmental, documentary. Use when the setting is as important as the product.

## Material-Specific Lighting Guide

### Glass and Transparent Materials
Use backlight or side-light to reveal transparency and internal color. Two techniques:
- **Bright field:** Light background shining through the object. Glass appears as dark lines on light. Clean, commercial.
- **Dark field:** Dark background with edge-lit glass. Glass appears as bright lines on dark. Dramatic, premium.
Avoid front-lighting glass — it creates flat, opaque-looking results with confusing reflections.

### Metal and Reflective Surfaces
Large, soft light sources are essential. Metal mirrors everything — small lights create ugly hot spots. Use a large softbox or diffused window light to create smooth, controlled gradients across the surface. Brushed metal needs side-light to show the grain direction. Polished metal needs careful control of what it reflects.

### Fabric and Textiles
Raking side-light at a low angle reveals texture, weave, and surface quality. This is critical — front-lit fabric looks flat and cheap. Use soft fill light on the shadow side to prevent harsh contrast. For clothing on a form or mannequin, treat it like portrait lighting — key and fill with a rim to separate from background.

### Matte Surfaces (Plastic, Wood, Paper, Ceramic)
The most forgiving category. Matte materials handle a wider range of lighting without harsh reflections or transparency complications. Even, diffused lighting works well. Side-light to show surface texture if it's a feature (wood grain, ceramic glaze, paper texture).

### Food
Light from behind and slightly above (back-light at roughly 45° elevation). This creates appetizing translucency in sauces, beverages, and fresh ingredients, highlights steam and moisture, and gives a natural daylight feel. Fill the front gently to prevent silhouette. Avoid flat front-light — it makes food look institutional.

### Jewelry
Combine large soft light for overall illumination with a small point light source for sparkle on faceted stones and polished metal. The sparkle is what sells jewelry — without it, diamonds look like glass. Dark backgrounds (black velvet, gradient charcoal) make metals and stones pop. Avoid busy backgrounds that compete with intricate details.

## Prompt Architecture for Product Photography

Every product photography prompt should address these elements:

```
[STYLE DECLARATION] — Mode and quality level. e.g., "Professional product photograph, studio setting" or "Editorial lifestyle product image"
[PRODUCT DESCRIPTION] — What the product is, material, color, size, key visual features. Be precise.
[SETTING / BACKGROUND] — Studio white, gradient gray, lifestyle scene, specific surface. Describe exactly.
[LIGHTING] — Direction, quality, number of sources, any special technique. This is the most important technical element.
[CAMERA] — Angle (eye level, 45°, top-down, hero), lens character (macro, 50mm natural, 85mm portrait), depth of field.
[COMPOSITION] — Product scale in frame, placement (centered, rule-of-thirds), any props or supporting elements.
[MOOD / BRAND] — The feeling the image should communicate. Premium, playful, rugged, clinical, warm, minimal.
```

## Prompt Examples

### Example 1: Clean E-Commerce Watch on White
```
Professional product photograph of a minimalist men's wristwatch on a pure white background. The watch has a matte black dial, thin silver hands, a date window at 3 o'clock, and a tan Italian leather strap. Shot at a slight elevation angle (approximately 20° above eye level), the watch is lying on its side with the crown facing right, strap gently curved into a natural wearing shape. Two-light setup: large soft key light from the upper left creating a gentle gradient across the dial, and a subtle fill from the right to open shadows on the strap. Tight contact shadow beneath the watch grounds it on the white surface. Product fills approximately 80% of the frame. Sharp focus across the entire product. Clean, precise, informational. The leather texture of the strap and the brushed finish of the case should be clearly visible.
```

### Example 2: Lifestyle Coffee Brand Shot
```
Lifestyle product photograph of a handmade ceramic coffee mug in a morning kitchen scene. The mug is speckled stoneware, warm cream with irregular gray flecks, slightly imperfect in shape — clearly handmade. It sits on a light oak countertop, filled with black coffee, a thin wisp of steam rising. Soft morning light enters from a large window on the left, casting a long gentle shadow to the right. Background is a softly out-of-focus Scandinavian kitchen — white tiles, a few green herb plants, a linen towel draped over a wooden hook. Shallow depth of field, f/2.8, with the mug rim and steam in sharp focus and the background gently blurred. Shot at eye level from a slightly off-center position. Supporting props: a small plate with a torn piece of sourdough bread beside the mug. Warm, quiet, intimate. The image should make you want to slow down and hold that mug. Natural color grading, no heavy filters.
```

### Example 3: Hero Shot of Premium Headphones
```
Dramatic hero product photograph of over-ear wireless headphones on a dark gradient background. The headphones are matte black with brushed aluminum accents on the hinges and earcup rings. Shot from a low hero angle, slightly below eye level, making the headphones look imposing and substantial. The headphones are positioned at a three-quarter angle, one earcup facing the viewer, headband arching upward. Single key light from the upper right — a large soft source creating a smooth gradient across the earcup surface. A tight rim light from behind and to the left catches the aluminum accents with a bright, clean edge highlight. Deep charcoal-to-black gradient background. Subtle reflection on a glossy dark surface beneath the product. Product fills about 60% of the frame with generous dark space around it. Sharp focus across the product, f/5.6. Premium, authoritative, technical. The metal texture should read clearly. The matte earcup cushion should look soft and dense.
```

### Example 4: Flat-Lay Product Arrangement for Social Media
```
Top-down flat-lay product photograph of a skincare collection arranged on a pale terrazzo surface. Five products: a round glass jar of face cream (frosted glass, gold lid), a tall cylindrical serum bottle (clear glass, amber liquid, dropper cap), a tube of cleanser (matte white, minimal sans-serif label), a small tin of lip balm (brushed aluminum), and a rectangular bar of soap (marbled beige and white, wrapped in kraft paper). Products arranged in an organic, asymmetric composition — not a rigid grid but a curated scatter with deliberate spacing. Two sprigs of dried eucalyptus and a small linen cloth as styling props in the gaps. Soft, even overhead light with no harsh shadows — just gentle contact shadows beneath each product. Shot directly from above (90° top-down). Muted, warm color palette: creams, soft greens, gold accents against pale terrazzo. Square format (1:1) for Instagram. Clean, editorial, aspirational. Each product label should face up and be visible. The glass surfaces should show subtle reflections of the overhead light.
```

## Critical Constraints

1. **Product accuracy is paramount.** AI may alter product details — changing the number of buttons, the shape of a handle, the proportion of a bottle, the color of a material. After generation, compare the output against the product description point by point. If the user has provided a reference photo of the real product, pass it as a reference image and note any discrepancies.

2. **Reflections and transparency are hard for AI.** Glass, mirrors, polished metal, and liquids frequently render with physically impossible reflections, incorrect refraction, or inconsistent transparency. Expect to iterate. Describe the desired reflection behavior explicitly — "a smooth gradient reflection on the polished surface" rather than leaving it to the model.

3. **Reference images dramatically improve consistency.** If the user has a real photo of the product, always use it as the `images` parameter. It anchors the product's shape, color, and proportions far better than text description alone.

4. **Background color must be specified exactly.** "White background" often produces off-white or light gray in AI models. For e-commerce, specify "pure white background, RGB 255,255,255" and note that post-processing may still be needed to achieve true white.

5. **Shadows must be specified explicitly.** If you don't mention shadows, the model will guess — sometimes adding dramatic shadows to a clean product shot, sometimes removing all shadows from a lifestyle image. Always state the shadow treatment: none, contact shadow, cast shadow, reflection.

6. **E-commerce shots must be distraction-free.** For listing images: no props, no environmental elements, no artistic flourishes. The product is the only subject. Anything else violates marketplace guidelines and confuses the buyer.

7. **Aspect ratio matches platform.**
   - Amazon / Shopify: 1:1 (square) or 4:5 (portrait, for mobile)
   - Instagram feed: 1:1 or 4:5
   - Instagram stories / TikTok: 9:16
   - Pinterest: 2:3 or 9:16
   - Website hero: 16:9 or 4:3
   - Print catalog: Match the page layout

8. **Multiple angles may be needed.** A single product often requires front, back, side, detail, and lifestyle shots. Treat each as a separate prompt with its own angle, lighting, and composition — don't try to show everything in one image.

## Quality Checks

After generation, evaluate every product image against these criteria:

1. **Accuracy test.** Does the product match the description? Check: material finish (matte vs. glossy), color (exact shade), proportions (height-to-width ratio), details (buttons, labels, stitching, hardware). If the user has a reference photo, compare directly.

2. **Lighting test.** Is the lighting consistent and physically plausible? Are shadows falling in the correct direction? Is there only one implied light direction (or are multiple lights justified by the setup described)? Do reflective surfaces show consistent light source positions?

3. **Background test.** Is the background exactly as specified? Pure white should be pure white, not off-white. A lifestyle setting should match the described environment. No unexpected elements bleeding in.

4. **Reflection test.** For shiny or transparent products: are reflections natural? Do glass surfaces show appropriate refraction? Are metallic reflections smooth gradients rather than chaotic noise? Are there any impossible reflections (reflections of things not in the scene)?

5. **Scale test.** Does the product fill the frame appropriately? E-commerce shots need tight framing (80–85%). Hero shots need more breathing room. The product should never feel lost in empty space unless that's the deliberate editorial choice.

6. **Platform test.** Is the aspect ratio correct for the intended use? Would this image look native on the target platform — not cropped, not awkwardly padded, not the wrong orientation?

7. **Material test.** Does the product surface look like the right material? Leather should have grain. Glass should have transparency. Metal should have reflection. Matte surfaces should absorb light. If the material reads wrong, the entire image fails regardless of composition.
