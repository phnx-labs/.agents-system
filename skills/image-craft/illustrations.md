# Illustrations

Character design, scene illustration, concept art, infographic illustrations, storyboards, spot illustrations, editorial art.

---

## Understanding Phase

Before generating an illustration, extract these from the user's request:

- **Subject** — What or who is being illustrated. Get specifics: species, age, clothing, expression, posture, environment. Vague subjects produce generic results.
- **Narrative context** — What story does this illustration serve? A character portrait for a children's book has different needs than a concept art piece for a game pitch.
- **Intended use** — Where will this appear? Book interior, blog header, app icon, poster, social post, presentation, product packaging. Use determines size, detail level, and style.
- **Style preference** — Does the user have a specific style in mind? Reference artists, existing works, or style categories. If unclear, recommend based on the use case.
- **Audience** — Children, teenagers, professionals, general public. Audience determines complexity, tone, and content appropriateness.
- **Technical requirements** — Background (transparent, white, scene), orientation (portrait, landscape, square), color mode (full color, monochrome, limited palette).

Illustrations serve a purpose. Every illustration does one or more of these jobs:
- **Explain** — Make complex information visual and accessible (infographics, diagrams, technical illustration).
- **Decorate** — Add visual interest and personality to content (spot illustrations, borders, patterns).
- **Narrate** — Tell or advance a story (scene illustration, sequential art, storyboards).
- **Emotionalize** — Create an emotional response (editorial illustration, concept art, character portraits).

Identify the primary job before selecting style and composing the prompt.

## Illustration Style Categories

### 1. Photorealistic / Hyperreal
Painterly detail approaching or surpassing photography. Rich textures, accurate lighting, anatomical precision, environmental detail.

**Use for**: Concept art, book interiors (adult), editorial features, game art, film pre-visualization.
**Key principles**: Accurate anatomy and proportions, realistic light behavior (bounce light, subsurface scattering, ambient occlusion), material differentiation (metal vs. fabric vs. skin vs. wood), atmospheric perspective for depth.
**Prompt cues**: "photorealistic illustration, hyper-detailed, cinematic lighting, realistic textures and materials, painterly rendering, anatomically accurate"

### 2. Flat / Vector
Clean geometric shapes, solid colors, no gradients or minimal gradients. Crisp edges, limited palette. Information-forward.

**Use for**: Infographics, UI/UX illustration, corporate communications, icon sets, explainer content, app onboarding.
**Key principles**: Geometric simplicity, limited color palette (4-6 colors max), clear silhouettes, consistent stroke weight if outlines are used, information hierarchy through size and color.
**Prompt cues**: "flat vector illustration, clean geometric shapes, solid colors, minimal design, limited color palette, crisp edges, modern corporate illustration style"

### 3. Hand-Drawn / Sketch
Visible pen, pencil, or charcoal strokes. Imperfect lines, organic feel, human touch. Can range from loose gestural sketches to tight pen-and-ink work.

**Use for**: Editorial illustration, children's books, personal branding, journals, indie publishing, zines.
**Key principles**: Intentional imperfection (lines that don't perfectly connect, varied pressure), warmth and humanity, visible process (sketch lines showing through), personality in line quality.
**Prompt cues**: "hand-drawn illustration, visible pencil strokes, organic imperfect lines, sketchy quality, warm and human feel, traditional media aesthetic"

### 4. Watercolor / Painterly
Soft edges, color bleeding and blooming, visible brushwork, translucent layering. Organic color mixing with happy accidents.

**Use for**: Book illustration (children's and literary), fine art prints, greeting cards, editorial, botanical illustration.
**Key principles**: Transparency and layering (lighter washes build up to darker values), wet-into-wet effects for soft transitions, dry brush for texture, paper texture showing through, unpredictable color bleeding.
**Prompt cues**: "watercolor illustration, soft bleeding edges, translucent color washes, visible paper texture, painterly brushstrokes, organic color mixing"

### 5. Isometric / Technical
3D objects rendered at a consistent isometric angle (30° from horizontal). No perspective distortion — parallel lines remain parallel. Clean, informational, precise.

**Use for**: Tech explainers, architectural visualization, product documentation, infographic scenes, workspace/office illustrations, city/map views.
**Key principles**: Consistent 30° isometric angle throughout, no vanishing points, clean edges with optional thin outlines, consistent light source direction, modular building-block composition.
**Prompt cues**: "isometric illustration, 30-degree angle, no perspective distortion, clean technical rendering, modular composition, consistent lighting direction, informational clarity"

### 6. Cartoon / Character
Exaggerated proportions, expressive faces, simplified forms, appeal-driven design. Characters communicate emotion through pose, expression, and shape language.

**Use for**: Children's content, mobile apps, brand mascots, explainer videos, educational material, comics.
**Key principles**: Shape language (round = friendly, angular = edgy, square = stable), exaggerated expressions readable at small sizes, consistent character model sheets (head-to-body ratio, feature proportions), appeal through simplification.
**Prompt cues**: "cartoon character illustration, exaggerated proportions, expressive face, simplified forms, appealing character design, bold outlines, [round/angular/square] shape language"

### 7. Collage / Mixed Media
Combines photography, texture, illustration, and typography. Layered, textural, often deconstructed. The juxtaposition of media types creates visual tension and interest.

**Use for**: Editorial illustration, music/album art, experimental branding, poster design, zine culture.
**Key principles**: Intentional layering with varied opacity and blend modes, contrast between organic and mechanical elements, paper texture and torn edges, deliberate roughness and imperfection, visual surprise through unexpected combinations.
**Prompt cues**: "mixed media collage illustration, layered textures, combination of photography and illustration, torn paper edges, vintage ephemera, textural contrast, experimental composition"

### 8. Pixel Art / Retro
Grid-based artwork at limited resolution. Each pixel is placed intentionally. Nostalgic, referencing early digital culture and gaming.

**Use for**: Gaming, retro branding, tech culture content, social media avatars, indie game assets.
**Key principles**: Strict grid discipline, limited color palette (8-32 colors typical), readable at native resolution, anti-aliasing through manual pixel placement, dithering for gradients, clear silhouettes.
**Prompt cues**: "pixel art illustration, grid-based design, limited color palette, retro gaming aesthetic, clean pixel placement, nostalgic 8-bit/16-bit style"

### 9. Line Art / Ink
Pure line work with no fills or very limited fills. Emphasis on contour, cross-hatching, stippling, and line weight variation to create form and tone.

**Use for**: Technical illustration, editorial, adult coloring books, tattoo design, architectural sketches, botanical illustration.
**Key principles**: Line weight variation (thick outlines, thin internal details), cross-hatching and stippling for tonal range, clean line intersections, negative space as a compositional element, ink-on-paper aesthetic.
**Prompt cues**: "ink line art illustration, pure line work, varied line weights, cross-hatching for tone, clean pen and ink style, high contrast black on white"

## Character Design Specifics

When the illustration involves characters, additional detail is required:

### Shape Language
Characters communicate personality through their fundamental shapes:
- **Circles/Rounds** — Friendly, approachable, soft, innocent. For: protagonists, children, comedic characters.
- **Triangles/Angles** — Dynamic, dangerous, edgy, villainous. For: antagonists, action characters, tricksters.
- **Squares/Rectangles** — Stable, strong, reliable, stubborn. For: authority figures, guardians, dependable sidekicks.

Mix shapes for complex characters. A hero might have a round head (approachable) on a square body (strong).

### Expression and Pose
- Describe expressions specifically: "worried frown with one eyebrow raised" beats "looking concerned."
- Pose communicates as much as face. Describe the full body posture: slouching, leaning forward, arms crossed, mid-stride.
- Action poses: describe the peak of the action (the highest point of a jump, the moment of impact, the wind-up before a throw).

### Consistency Across Multiple Images
AI struggles to reproduce the same character across generations. To maintain consistency:
- Write a detailed character spec: proportions, distinctive features, color palette, clothing details.
- Use reference images from earlier generations as input for subsequent ones.
- Keep descriptions identical across prompts — copy-paste character descriptions verbatim.
- Focus on distinctive, reproducible features: an unusual hat, a scar, a color combination, an exaggerated proportion.

## Composition Principles for Illustration

### Rule of Thirds
Place focal points at the intersection of an imaginary 3×3 grid. Avoid dead-center placement unless symmetry is intentional.

### Visual Hierarchy
Guide the viewer's eye through the illustration:
1. **Size** — Larger elements are seen first.
2. **Contrast** — High-contrast areas attract attention before low-contrast areas.
3. **Color** — Saturated or warm colors advance; desaturated or cool colors recede.
4. **Detail** — Areas of high detail draw focus over areas of low detail.
5. **Isolation** — An element surrounded by empty space demands attention.

### Depth Techniques
- **Overlapping** — Objects in front partially obscure objects behind.
- **Scale diminution** — Distant objects are smaller.
- **Atmospheric perspective** — Distant objects lose contrast, shift toward blue/gray, lose detail.
- **Focus/blur** — Sharp foreground, soft background (or vice versa for dramatic effect).

### Negative Space
Empty space is a compositional tool, not wasted space. Use it to:
- Direct attention to the subject.
- Create breathing room and elegance.
- Leave space for text overlay if the illustration accompanies copy.
- Establish mood (vast emptiness = isolation; tight framing = claustrophobia).

## Prompt Architecture

Structure every illustration prompt with these components:

```
[STYLE DECLARATION] — Declare the illustration style explicitly and specifically.
  e.g. "Flat vector illustration in a modern tech-corporate style"

[SUBJECT] — Describe what/who is being illustrated with specific, concrete detail.
  e.g. "A woman in her 30s wearing a dark blue blazer, sitting cross-legged on the floor surrounded by open books and scattered papers, reading intently with a slight smile"

[COMPOSITION] — Arrangement, scale, perspective, focal point, negative space.
  e.g. "Three-quarter view, subject centered in the lower two-thirds, books radiating outward, ample negative space above for header text"

[COLOR PALETTE] — Specific palette with hex codes or clear direction.
  e.g. "Warm limited palette: cream background (#FFF8F0), navy blazer (#1B2A4A), terracotta accents (#C4634F), soft sage books (#9CAF88)"

[LINE QUALITY] — If relevant to the style: thick/thin, clean/rough, uniform/varied.
  e.g. "Clean uniform 2px outlines with no variation, rounded line caps"

[DETAIL LEVEL] — Simplified/minimal vs. moderately detailed vs. highly intricate.
  e.g. "Moderately detailed — individual books have visible covers but no text, facial features are simplified to geometric shapes"

[MOOD/TONE] — Emotional register of the piece.
  e.g. "Warm, intellectual, quietly joyful, cozy"

[REFERENCE DIRECTION] — Artist, style movement, or published work reference. Optional but powerful.
  e.g. "In the style of modern editorial illustration, similar to the work seen in Monocle or The New Yorker"
```

```
Watercolor children's book illustration, portrait 3:4 format. A small red fox kit sitting on a mossy log in a sun-dappled forest clearing, head tilted to one side with large curious eyes and one ear perked up. The fox has an oversized head relative to its body (roughly 1:2 ratio), soft round features, and a bushy tail curled around its paws. ```

```
Isometric illustration of a creative workspace, square 1:1 format. A detailed isometric room viewed from the standard 30-degree angle showing: a large wooden desk with a monitor, keyboard, and scattered art supplies; a bookshelf against the back wall filled with colorful book spines and small plants; a cat sleeping on a round cushion on the floor; a window with warm afternoon light casting geometric shadows; a cork board with pinned sketches on the side wall; a coffee mug with steam rising. ### Hand-Drawn Editorial — Magazine Article
```
Hand-drawn editorial illustration for a magazine article about the hidden cost of constant connectivity, portrait 3:4 format. A person sitting at a cafe table, hunched over a smartphone, while around them the cafe scene dissolves into a swirling vortex of notification icons, message bubbles, clock faces, and tangled charging cables — the physical world fragmenting into digital noise. The person is rendered in detailed pen and ink with cross-hatching for shadow, while the swirling digital elements are looser, more frantic, sketched with hurried aggressive strokes. Monochrome with one accent color: all black ink line work on white (#FFFFFF) except notification dots and screen glow in electric blue (#0077FF). Varied line weights — heavy 3px outlines on the person, 1px internal details, scratchy 0.5px lines for the disintegrating background. Subject seated in the center, digital vortex radiating outward to all edges. Mood: anxious, critical, thought-provoking, visually overwhelming by design. Upper portion of the image should have slightly less density, leaving space for article headline overlay.
```

## Critical Constraints

### Style Consistency in Series
When producing multiple illustrations for a single project (book, app, campaign), consistency is paramount:
- Document the style spec after the first successful generation: palette, line weight, detail level, character proportions, background treatment.
- Copy this spec verbatim into every subsequent prompt.
- Use reference images from previous generations as input to anchor the style.
- Resist the temptation to "improve" the style mid-series. Consistency beats individual excellence.

### Hands, Faces, and Anatomy
AI generation struggles with:
- **Hands** — Extra fingers, merged fingers, impossible poses. Mitigate: describe hand positions precisely ("right hand gripping a coffee mug handle, four fingers visible and thumb wrapped around"), or design compositions where hands are obscured, in pockets, or out of frame.
- **Faces** — Asymmetry, uncanny expressions, inconsistent features across images. Mitigate: describe expressions precisely, use styles where faces are simplified (cartoon, flat vector), or frame subjects from behind or in profile.
- **Complex anatomy** — Foreshortened limbs, seated poses, interacting figures. Mitigate: describe the spatial relationships explicitly, use simple poses, avoid extreme foreshortening.

### Information Clarity in Infographic Illustrations
When the illustration's job is to explain:
- Clarity of information always overrides artistic style.
- Every visual element must serve comprehension. Decorative elements that confuse the hierarchy are harmful.
- Use size, color, and position to create a clear reading order.
- Labels and annotations should be added in post-processing — do not rely on AI-generated text.

### Children's Illustration Standards
When illustrating for children:
- **Appeal** is the highest priority. Characters must be likable and approachable.
- **Expression** must be readable and unambiguous. A child should immediately understand what the character is feeling.
- Avoid anything frightening, violent, or anatomically inappropriate unless specifically requested for age-appropriate storytelling.
- **Diversity** in characters should be natural and present by default.

### Text in Illustrations
AI-rendered text within illustrations is unreliable. For any illustration that needs labels, titles, captions, or integrated text:
- Design the composition with clear zones for text to be added in post-processing.
- Describe placeholder elements: "a blank sign," "an empty speech bubble," "a book with a visible but blank cover."
- Never rely on the AI to produce readable text within the illustration.

### Reproduction and Scale
Consider the final output medium:
- **Large format** (posters, murals): Can include fine detail and subtle texture.
- **Medium format** (book interiors, editorial): Moderate detail, clear silhouettes.
- **Small format** (icons, thumbnails, social media): Must work as simple shapes with high contrast. Eliminate fine detail.
- **Screen vs. print**: Screen illustrations can use any color; print illustrations should avoid colors outside the CMYK gamut (neon, pure cyan, electric hues).

## Quality Checks

Run these checks before finalizing any illustration prompt:

1. **Style consistency test** — If this is part of a series, does the prompt maintain the same style spec (palette, line weight, detail level, character proportions) as previous pieces?
2. **Purpose test** — Does this illustration serve its intended function? If it's supposed to explain, does it clarify? If it's supposed to emotionalize, does it evoke the right feeling?
3. **Audience test** — Is the style, content, and complexity appropriate for the target audience? A corporate infographic should not look like a children's book; a children's illustration should not look clinical.
4. **Detail calibration test** — Is the level of detail appropriate for both the style and the reproduction size? Too much detail in a small-format illustration muddies the image; too little in a large format looks sparse.
5. **Character test** — If characters are present: Are proportions consistent? Are expressions clear and readable? Is shape language intentional? Would someone want to spend time with this character?
6. **Composition test** — Does the arrangement guide the eye to the right place? Is there appropriate negative space? Is the focal point clear?
7. **Reproduction test** — Will this work at its intended size and medium? Will fine details survive? Will colors translate if printed?
8. **Hands and faces check** — Have you described hand positions and facial expressions with enough precision to minimize AI artifacts? Have you considered simplifying or obscuring problematic areas?
