# Logos & Brand Marks

Sub-skill for logos, icons, app icons, monograms, brand symbols, and wordmarks. Use this when the deliverable is a mark that must represent an identity — compact, scalable, and instantly meaningful.

---

## Understanding Phase

Before designing anything, extract the brand's identity. A logo is a compressed argument about who this entity is.

### What to Extract

| Dimension | What to Look For | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Values** | What the brand stands for at its core — 2-3 words | Trust, speed, craft |
| **Personality** | If the brand were a person, how would they speak and carry themselves? | Calm and authoritative, playful and irreverent, precise and minimal |
| **Industry** | Sector and context — this determines visual conventions and opportunities to break them | Fintech, artisanal bakery, legal services, indie gaming |
| **Audience** | Who sees this logo most? Age, sophistication, expectations | Enterprise CTOs, teenagers, home cooks, venture capitalists |
| **Differentiation** | What makes this brand different from its direct competitors? | The only one that's open-source; the only one that's local; the fastest |
| **Feeling** | The single emotional response the logo should trigger in 0.5 seconds | Confidence, warmth, precision, rebellion, calm |

### Questions to Ask or Infer

If the user hasn't provided a full brief, infer answers from context or ask the most critical ones:

1. **What does the brand do?** (One sentence — if you can't say it in one sentence, the logo can't say it in one mark.)
2. **What feeling should someone get when they see this logo?** (Not "professional" — that's a minimum. What *specific* feeling?)
3. **Where will this logo live?** (App icon, website header, business card, storefront sign, all of the above?)
4. **Are there existing brand colors or is the palette open?**
5. **Does the logo need to include text, or is a symbol/icon sufficient?**
6. **What logos does the client admire? What do they hate?** (Even one example in each direction reveals more than a paragraph of brief.)

### Mood vs. Meaning

A logo must convey both simultaneously:
- **Mood** is the feeling — warm, sharp, luxurious, approachable, brutal. It comes from shape language, color, weight, and whitespace.
- **Meaning** is the what — this is a law firm, this is a coffee shop, this makes developer tools. It comes from symbol choice, typography, and visual convention.

If the mood is right but meaning is wrong (a playful, rounded logo for a defense contractor), it fails. If the meaning is right but the mood is wrong (a military-stencil font for a children's toy brand), it fails. Both must align.

## Logo Style Categories

### 1. Flat / Minimalist

**Description:** Two-dimensional, no gradients or shadows. Clean geometric shapes, limited color palette (often 1-2 colors plus white). Relies on precision, proportion, and negative space.

**Best for:** Tech companies, SaaS products, modern consumer brands, startups that want to signal sophistication.

**Examples to study:** Nike swoosh, Airbnb, Spotify, Stripe, Medium, Notion.

**Design principles:**
- Geometric proportion matters — use golden ratio relationships, 45° and 90° angles, mathematically derived curves.
- Negative space is a design element, not leftover space. The FedEx arrow, the Spartan Golf Club golfer — the best flat logos encode a second reading in the whitespace.
- Color must be functional. Each color in the palette must do a job. If you can remove a color without losing anything, remove it.
- Test at extremes: the logo must be instantly readable at 16×16 pixels (favicon) and still feel intentional at billboard scale.

**Prompt keywords:** `flat vector logo`, `geometric`, `minimalist`, `clean lines`, `solid color`, `no gradients`, `no shadows`, `negative space`.

### 2. 3D / Dimensional

**Description:** Uses gradients, shadows, reflections, and depth to create the illusion of a three-dimensional object. Can range from subtle depth (a soft gradient on a shield shape) to full rendered 3D (a glossy sphere with realistic lighting).

**Best for:** Gaming studios, entertainment brands, premium/luxury products, brands that want to feel tactile or substantial.

**Examples to study:** PlayStation, Xbox, Firefox, Instagram (current gradient icon), Apple (earlier eras).

**Design principles:**
- Commit to a single, consistent light source. Mixed lighting looks amateurish.
- Material quality defines the mood — matte feels premium, glossy feels energetic, metallic feels authoritative.
- Restrain the effects. One gradient direction. One shadow angle. One reflection type. Stacking effects creates visual noise.
- Ensure a flat fallback exists. Any 3D logo must degrade gracefully to a flat version for contexts where depth doesn't render (embroidery, engraving, single-color print).

**Prompt keywords:** `3D logo`, `gradient`, `dimensional`, `depth`, `shadow`, `glossy`, `matte finish`, `metallic`, `light source from [direction]`.

### 3. Wordmark / Logotype

**Description:** The brand name itself is the logo, rendered in custom or carefully chosen typography. No separate icon — the letterforms carry all the identity.

**Best for:** Brands with distinctive names, fashion labels, media companies, any brand where the name is the primary asset.

**Examples to study:** Google, Coca-Cola, FedEx, Supreme, VOGUE, CNN, Disney.

**Design principles:**
- Custom letterforms beat off-the-shelf fonts. Even small modifications — a trimmed ascender, a rounded terminal, a connected ligature — create ownership.
- Kerning is everything. Letter spacing defines whether the wordmark feels tight and urgent or open and calm.
- Weight communicates personality: ultra-light = elegant/fragile; bold = confident/loud; medium = balanced/reliable.
- The wordmark must work as a single unit — it should feel like one shape, not a row of individual letters.

**Prompt keywords:** `wordmark logo`, `logotype`, `custom typography`, `lettering`, `[weight: bold/light/medium]`, `[style: serif/sans-serif/script]`, `[case: uppercase/lowercase/title case]`.

### 4. Lettermark / Monogram

**Description:** One to three initials designed as a unified mark. Often interlocking, overlapping, or geometrically composed.

**Best for:** Companies with long names, law firms, consulting firms, fashion houses, any context where initials are already in common use.

**Examples to study:** IBM, HBO, CNN, Louis Vuitton (LV), Chanel (CC), HP, GE.

**Design principles:**
- The letters must read as one form, not separate characters placed next to each other. Interlocking, nesting, or sharing structural elements (a shared vertical stroke, a shared counter) achieves this.
- Negative space between and within the letterforms creates the mark's visual interest.
- Readability at small sizes is non-negotiable. If the letters become ambiguous at 16px, simplify.
- Symmetry and balance matter more in monograms than in most other logo types.

**Prompt keywords:** `lettermark logo`, `monogram`, `interlocking letters`, `geometric initials`, `[the letters]`, `unified mark`.

### 5. Symbol / Icon

**Description:** An abstract or pictorial mark with no text. A standalone shape that represents the brand entirely through visual meaning.

**Best for:** Global brands (bypasses language), tech companies, app icons, any brand confident enough to stand on a symbol alone.

**Examples to study:** Apple, Twitter/X, Target, Shell, Nike (swoosh alone), Mercedes-Benz, Playboy.

**Design principles:**
- Simplicity is survival. The symbol must work at 16×16 pixels. If it has more than 3-4 visual elements, it's too complex.
- Meaning compression: the best symbols encode the brand's core idea in a single visual gesture. Apple: knowledge, simplicity, a bite of something forbidden. Target: precision, directness.
- Test the silhouette. Fill the logo solid black. If the shape is still distinctive and recognizable, it works. If it becomes an ambiguous blob, it doesn't.
- Avoid literal depictions of the product. A software company's logo shouldn't be a computer screen. Abstraction scales better than illustration.

**Prompt keywords:** `symbol logo`, `icon`, `abstract mark`, `pictorial mark`, `standalone symbol`, `no text`, `silhouette`.

### 6. Emblem

**Description:** Text enclosed within or tightly integrated with a symbol or shape — badge-like, often with a border or container. Feels institutional, established, weighty.

**Best for:** Universities, government agencies, heritage brands, breweries, sports teams, automotive companies, organizations with history.

**Examples to study:** Harley-Davidson, Starbucks, Harvard, NFL, BMW, Porsche.

**Design principles:**
- Symmetry (often circular or shield-shaped) creates the sense of authority.
- Detail balance is the central tension: emblems want to be detailed (that's their charm), but excessive detail destroys scalability. Design for the smallest intended use case first, then add detail for larger formats.
- The container shape (circle, shield, crest, banner) carries meaning. Circles = unity, community. Shields = protection, heritage. Banners = proclamation, tradition.
- Typography must integrate seamlessly with the surrounding artwork — matching weight, style, and era.

**Prompt keywords:** `emblem logo`, `badge`, `crest`, `seal`, `enclosed`, `circular`, `shield`, `vintage emblem`, `[container shape]`.

### 7. Hand-Drawn / Artisanal

**Description:** Organic, imperfect, crafted feel. Looks like it was drawn by a human hand — ink, brush, chalk, pencil. Intentional roughness signals authenticity.

**Best for:** Food and beverage brands, boutique businesses, creative agencies, craft products, anything that wants to feel handmade, local, or personal.

**Examples to study:** Mailchimp (Freddie), Innocent Drinks, craft brewery logos, artisanal food packaging.

**Design principles:**
- Imperfection must be intentional, not sloppy. The line wobbles, but the composition is controlled. The ink bleeds, but the proportions are deliberate.
- Warmth comes from organic shapes — no perfect circles, no mathematically straight lines, no pixel-perfect symmetry.
- Texture is critical: specify the medium (ink, brush, charcoal, chalk, woodcut, linocut, pencil). The rendering tool defines the personality.
- These logos often include more illustrative detail than other styles and that's acceptable — the hand-drawn quality gives them permission to be complex.

**Prompt keywords:** `hand-drawn logo`, `artisanal`, `ink illustration`, `brush lettering`, `hand-lettered`, `organic`, `crafted`, `woodcut`, `linocut`, `[medium: ink/brush/chalk/pencil]`.

## Prompt Architecture for Logos

Every logo prompt follows this skeleton:

```
[STYLE DECLARATION]
The logo style from the categories above. Be explicit.
"Flat minimalist vector logo design" not just "logo."

[SUBJECT / SYMBOL]
What the mark depicts or represents. Be specific about the visual form.
"A stylized mountain peak formed from three overlapping triangles" not "a mountain logo."

[DESIGN CONSTRAINTS]
Colors: specify exact palette ("deep navy #1B2A4A and white") or strict limits ("monochrome black on white").
Shapes: geometric vs. organic, angular vs. rounded, symmetrical vs. asymmetric.
Negative space: how it's used, what it reveals.

[TYPOGRAPHY] (if text is included)
Font style: serif, sans-serif, slab-serif, script, hand-lettered.
Weight: thin, light, regular, medium, bold, black.
Case: UPPERCASE, lowercase, Title Case.
Spacing: tight, normal, wide-tracked.
Position: below the mark, beside it, integrated into it.

[TECHNICAL SPECS]
"Clean vector edges, scalable from favicon to billboard."
"Isolated on [white/black/transparent] background."
"Works in single-color reproduction."

[REFERENCE DIRECTION]
Design era, movement, or aesthetic direction.
"Swiss International Style precision" or "Mid-century modern warmth"
or "Contemporary Silicon Valley minimalism."
```

## Prompt Examples

### Flat Minimalist Tech Logo

> Flat minimalist vector logo design. A stylized compass needle formed from two overlapping triangles — one pointing up (deep blue), one pointing down (teal) — creating a diamond shape at their intersection with negative space forming an arrow pointing right. Two colors only: deep blue #1A3A5C and teal #2AA89A on white background. Clean geometric construction, 45-degree angles, mathematically precise proportions. No gradients, no shadows, no outlines. Scalable from 16px favicon to large format. Isolated on clean white background. The aesthetic is contemporary Silicon Valley minimalism — Stripe, Linear, Vercel — where precision signals competence.

### 3D Dimensional Gaming Logo

> Three-dimensional metallic logo design with depth and lighting. A clenched fist breaking through a cracked stone surface, viewed from slightly below. The fist is brushed dark steel with subtle orange embers glowing through the cracks in the stone. Single dramatic light source from above-left casting a sharp shadow. The stone is textured granite, the cracks radiate outward with volumetric dust particles. Dark background — near black with a subtle warm gradient at the base. No text. The style is premium AAA game studio — Naughty Dog, Santa Monica Studio — where the mark feels like a physical object you could pick up. Isolated on dark background, clean edges.

### Artisanal Hand-Drawn Food Brand Logo

> Hand-drawn artisanal logo in black ink illustration style. A detailed but warm illustration of a sourdough loaf sitting in a traditional banneton basket, viewed from a three-quarter angle. Crosshatch shading on the bread crust, loose confident ink lines, visible pen texture. The brand name "STONE & GRAIN" is hand-lettered in a sturdy, slightly irregular uppercase serif beneath the illustration — the letters have the weight of carved stone but the warmth of a baker's hand. Single color: rich black ink on cream/off-white background. Feels like a woodcut from a century-old boulangerie — William Morris meets contemporary craft branding. Isolated on clean cream background.

### Monogram for Professional Service

> Lettermark monogram logo, clean and geometric. The letters "A" and "V" interlocked to form a single unified mark — the downstroke of the A and the left stroke of the V share a single line, creating a compact diamond-like form. Thin, precise lines — 1-2pt stroke weight — in dark charcoal #2C2C2C on white. No fill, outline only. The geometry is based on equilateral triangles. Below the mark, the firm name "ASHWORTH & VALE" in ultra-light uppercase sans-serif tracking set very wide. Monochrome. The aesthetic is high-end legal or architectural branding — Pentagram, Vignelli — where restraint signals authority. Isolated on white background, scalable to any size.

## Critical Constraints for Logos

### Text in AI-Generated Logos Is Unreliable

**This is the single most important constraint.** Current AI image models frequently garble, misspell, or distort letterforms. The longer the text, the worse the results.

**Best practice:**
- Generate the symbol/icon/mark without any text.
- Add the brand name, tagline, or any text in a proper design tool (Figma, Illustrator, Canva) after generation.
- If the user insists on text in the generated image, warn them that letter accuracy is not guaranteed and keep text to 1-3 characters maximum (initials are safer than full words).
- When text must be included in the prompt, spell it out explicitly and add: "The text must read exactly: [TEXT]" — this helps but does not guarantee accuracy.

### Always Specify Background

Every logo prompt must include one of:
- `"Isolated on clean white background"`
- `"Isolated on black background"`
- `"On transparent background"` (limited support)
- `"On [specific color] background"`

Without this, the model will invent a background — often an inappropriate textured or scenic one.

### Color Must Be Deliberate

- Specify exact hex codes when possible, or descriptive constraints ("monochrome," "two-color palette using only navy and gold").
- Limit to 2-3 colors maximum for most logo styles. More colors create logos that don't reproduce well in single-color contexts (engraving, embossing, fax).
- Consider how the logo looks in: full color, single color (black), reversed (white on dark), and grayscale.

### Scalability Is Non-Negotiable

- Include "scalable" or "works at all sizes" in every prompt.
- Mentally test: would this read as a 16×16 pixel favicon? As a 4-inch business card element? As a 40-foot building sign?
- Fine details (thin lines, small text, intricate patterns) vanish at small sizes. If the design requires them, it needs a simplified alternate version.

### Negative Space Matters

- Negative space is not empty — it's a design element. The space between and around elements shapes perception as much as the elements themselves.
- In the prompt, describe what the negative space does: "negative space between the two shapes forms an arrow," "the counter of the letter P contains a hidden leaf shape."
- Logos with no deliberate negative space usage look crowded and amateur.

## Quality Checks for Logos

Run every generated logo through these checks before presenting:

1. **Scalability test:** Mentally shrink this to 16×16 pixels. Are the essential shapes still distinguishable? Is it still recognizable? If fine details disappear and the mark loses its identity, the design is too complex.

2. **Simplicity test:** Could you draw this from memory after seeing it once? The best logos are simple enough to sketch on a napkin. If you can't hold the whole mark in your mind, simplify.

3. **Versatility test:** Does it work on a white background? A black background? A colored background? A photograph? If it only works in one context, it's not a real logo — it's an illustration.

4. **Text test:** If the logo contains text, examine every letterform. Are all letters correct and legible? Are there extra strokes, missing parts, or garbled characters? Flag any AI text issues immediately — this is the most common failure mode.

5. **Distinctiveness test:** Does this look like any well-known existing logo? Run a quick mental inventory of major brands in the same industry. If there's a strong resemblance, it's a problem — both legally and strategically.

6. **Industry fit test:** Does the style match the brand's sector and audience expectations? A hand-drawn watercolor logo for a cybersecurity firm creates cognitive dissonance. A rigid geometric emblem for a children's toy brand feels cold. Style must match context.

7. **Single-color test:** Imagine the logo printed in only black ink. Does it still work? Every professional logo needs to function in a single-color reproduction — for stamps, embossing, engraving, fax, and low-quality print.

8. **Memorability test:** After looking away for 10 seconds, what do you remember? If the answer is "a vague shape," the mark lacks a distinctive visual hook. Great logos have one defining gesture — the Nike swoosh, the Apple bite, the FedEx arrow — that anchors memory.
