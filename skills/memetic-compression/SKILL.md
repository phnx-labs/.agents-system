# Memetic Compression

Memetic compression is compressing complex cultural tensions into minimal words that resonate. The best memes borrow the emotional architecture of cultural artifacts — they don't reference them, they inhabit them.

## The Quality Bar

This is what we're aiming for:

> "You're the smartest sales rep I ever met... and you're too stupid to see they vibe coded a replacement CRM ten minutes ago"
>
> [Image: Hank from Breaking Bad, face-down, defeated]

Why it works:
- **Borrows emotional architecture**: Hank's face already carries exhaustion, dawning horror, the realization that something irreversible happened while you weren't looking. The audience hears "he made up his mind ten minutes ago" without it being written.
- **Rewrites the ACTUAL LINE**: Same structure as the original: compliment → reversal → knife twist. It's dialogue, not a caption.
- **"Ten minutes ago" is the knife**: The speed is the humiliation. Not "AI is coming" but "AI already did it while you were in the meeting."
- **No explanation**: Trusts the audience to complete the circuit.

## The Thinking Pattern

### Step 1: Find the Lived Tension

Not the headline version — the "4pm on Thursday" version. The feeling in the body. The specific moment.

| Headline (weak) | Lived experience (strong) |
|-----------------|---------------------------|
| "AI is disrupting sales" | "They vibe coded a replacement CRM ten minutes ago" |
| "AI affecting jobs" | "Mass-approved 12 PRs I didn't fully understand because we had to ship" |
| "Dating apps struggling" | "Opened ChatGPT to write a cover letter and now we have inside jokes" |
| "Entry-level hiring down" | "Spent 4 years learning to code. The entry-level job is 'review what Claude wrote'" |

Ask: What would someone text their friend who'd immediately understand?

### Step 2: Find the Cultural Artifact

Source material whose original emotional context already carries the weight.

The match must be **STRUCTURAL, not surface**. Ask:
- What does this mean to people who know it?
- What emotional weight does it carry BEFORE any words are added?
- Does the original dramatic arc map onto my tension?

**Good match (Dyatlov/Chernobyl for senior engineer approving PRs):**
- Original: Institutional pressure overriding the person who could've caught the problem
- Tension: Senior engineer approving AI-generated code under deadline pressure
- Both: Someone who should scrutinize but won't, impending disaster that looks fine right now

**Bad match (random popular meme for anything):**
- Surface-level: "This is popular, I'll use it"
- No structural resonance — just decoration

### Step 3: Write Minimum Language

Context-setting only: who, when, what happened. Then stop.

- No commentary
- No explaining the joke
- No quoting the source material (that's redundant — they already hear it)
- No specificity for cleverness

Test: Would someone who's lived this need you to say more? If yes, you've over-explained.

### Step 4: Pressure Test

Before shipping:
- Does the image do work the words also do? **Cut the words.**
- Are you completing the circuit for the reader? **Stop earlier.**
- Would you need to explain this to someone who's lived it? **Over-explained.**

## Two Formats

### Format A: Image + Rewritten Dialogue

Find a scene with an iconic face/moment. Rewrite the ACTUAL LINE, preserving the structure.

**The Breaking Bad structure:**
```
Compliment → Reversal → Temporal knife

"You're the smartest [X]... and you're too stupid to see [thing that already happened]"
```

The temporal knife ("ten minutes ago", "while you were in the meeting") is what makes it land. It's not "AI is coming" — it's "AI already did it."

**Other structures to consider:**
- Chernobyl: Confident dismissal before disaster
- Succession: Power dynamics and dismissiveness
- No Country for Old Men: Existential choice / random obsolescence
- The Office: Bureaucratic absurdity

### Format B: Image-less Flat Statement

When the sentence itself compresses enough, no artifact needed. The gap between setup and landing IS the joke.

```
"opened chatgpt to write a cover letter and now we have inside jokes"

"spent four years learning to code and the entry-level job is 'review what claude wrote'"

"got laid off for 'AI efficiencies' six months ago. checked in with old team. they're not using AI. they just stopped doing my job."
```

Flat delivery. No commentary. The reader does the math.

## Failure Modes

**Captions instead of memes**
- Weak: "Senior engineer after mass-approving 12 PRs"
- Strong: [Dyatlov at control panel, confident] + rewritten dialogue that inhabits the moment

Captions describe. Memes inhabit.

**Explaining the joke**
- Weak: Chernobyl image + "I'm told it's the equivalent of a chest x-ray"
- Strong: Chernobyl image + minimal context (you hear "not great not terrible" without it being written)

If you quote the source on the source image, you're redundant.

**Surface-level references**
- Picking artifacts because they're popular, not because they structurally match
- The artifact's original emotional context must do half the work

**Completing the circuit**
- The pleasure of a meme is recognition — the reader connecting it
- If you complete it for them, you've stolen that

**Observation instead of scene**
- Weak: Commentary ABOUT a tension ("this is what's happening")
- Strong: Dialogue WITHIN the moment (someone speaking TO the person)

## Research Process

When creating a meme:

1. **Search for what's live NOW** — tensions, anxieties, inversions, absurdities
2. **Find the texture, not the take** — what are people actually experiencing, not what headlines say
3. **Look for time ratios** — the sharper the contrast (4 years training vs. "ten minutes ago"), the harder it lands
4. **Identify 2-3 cultural artifacts** that structurally rhyme with each tension
5. **Write minimal version**, pressure test, iterate
6. **If it needs explaining, cut more**

## Quality Checklist

Before posting:

- [ ] Would I need to explain this to someone who's lived it?
- [ ] Does the image carry weight the words don't duplicate?
- [ ] Is there a temporal knife?
- [ ] Am I inhabiting the moment or observing it?
- [ ] Does the source material's original context amplify the joke?
- [ ] Did I resist the urge to add more?
