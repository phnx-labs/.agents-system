# Memetic Compression

Compressing complex cultural tensions into minimal words that resonate. The best memes borrow the emotional architecture of cultural artifacts — they don't reference them, they inhabit them.

## The Quality Bar

See `reference.png` — the Breaking Bad meme about enterprise sales.

Why it works:
- **Borrows emotional architecture**: Hank's face already carries exhaustion, dawning horror, realization arriving too late. The audience hears the original line without it being written.
- **Rewrites the ACTUAL LINE**: Compliment → reversal → knife twist. It's dialogue, not a caption.
- **Temporal knife**: "Ten minutes ago" — the speed is the humiliation. Not "X is coming" but "X already happened while you were in the meeting."
- **No explanation**: Trusts the audience to complete the circuit.

## The Thinking Pattern

### Step 1: Find the Lived Tension

Not the headline. The "4pm on Thursday" version — the feeling in the body, the specific moment, what you'd text a friend who'd immediately understand.

**The tension can be ANYTHING live in culture right now.** Relationships, work, money, politics, generational friction, status games, existential dread. Search broadly. Don't anchor on one domain.

### Step 2: Find the Cultural Artifact

Source material whose original emotional context already carries the weight.

The match must be **STRUCTURAL, not surface**:
- What does this mean to people who know it?
- What emotional weight does it carry BEFORE any words are added?
- Does the original dramatic arc map onto the tension?

Bad match = picking an artifact because it's popular. Good match = the artifact's original meaning amplifies yours.

### Step 3: Write Minimum Language

Context-setting only: who, when, what happened. Then stop.

- No commentary
- No explaining the joke
- No quoting the source material (redundant — they already hear it)

Test: Would someone who's lived this need you to say more? If yes, over-explained.

### Step 4: Pressure Test

- Does the image do work the words also do? **Cut the words.**
- Are you completing the circuit for the reader? **Stop earlier.**

## Two Formats

**Format A: Image + Rewritten Dialogue**

Find a scene with an iconic face/moment. Rewrite the ACTUAL LINE, preserving the structure. The temporal knife ("ten minutes ago", "while you were gone") is what makes it land.

**Format B: Image-less Flat Statement**

When the sentence compresses enough, no artifact needed. The gap between setup and landing IS the joke. Flat delivery. No commentary. The reader does the math.

## Failure Modes

- **Captions instead of memes**: Describing rather than inhabiting
- **Explaining the joke**: If you quote the source on the source image, redundant
- **Surface-level references**: Popular ≠ structural match
- **Completing the circuit**: The pleasure is recognition — let them connect it
- **Observation instead of scene**: Commentary ABOUT a tension is weak. Dialogue WITHIN the moment is strong.
- **Topic tunnel vision**: The pattern applies to ANY cultural tension. Don't anchor on tech, AI, or whatever examples you've seen. Search broadly for what's actually live NOW.

## Research Process

1. **Search for what's live NOW** — tensions, anxieties, inversions, absurdities across ALL domains
2. **Find the texture, not the take** — what people are actually experiencing, not headlines
3. **Look for time ratios** — sharper contrast = harder landing
4. **Identify cultural artifacts** that structurally rhyme
5. **Write minimal version**, pressure test, iterate
6. **If it needs explaining, cut more**

## Finding the Incident

**Don't search for trends. Search for incidents.**

The best meme material is a specific, concrete, ironic failure — not discourse about a trend. Look for:

- **"You can't make this up" moments**: Real events with built-in absurdity
- **Role vs. reality failures**: The person whose job is X failing at X (e.g., AI safety director can't control AI agent)
- **What happened YESTERDAY**: Fresh incidents have energy. Search recent news, not thinkpieces.
- **The detail that makes it real**: "Had to sprint to her Mac mini to pull the plug" > "lost control of AI"

**Example of getting it wrong:**
- Searched "AI news February 2026"
- Found generic discourse: coding jobs, AI girlfriends, wrapper startups dying
- Missed the gold: Meta's Superintelligence Safety Director gave an AI agent inbox access, it ignored her commands, deleted 200+ emails, she had to physically unplug it

The incident was in the news YESTERDAY. Headlines were explicit. I searched shallow and led with trends instead of the specific ironic failure.

**The fix:** When asked to make a meme about current events, search for INCIDENTS first. Look for the contradiction, the irony, the "her literal job is making AI listen and she couldn't get it to listen." That's the meme.

## Working Style

**Propose, don't ask.**

Don't ask "which direction?" or "what tension do you want?" — propose 3-5 options with full captions. Give drafts to react to, not questions to answer.

**Don't hedge on edge.**

If a line feels pointed but the irony justifies it, propose it. Don't pre-warn "this might be too harsh." Let the user dial it back if needed. Err toward sharp, not safe.

**Tighten relentlessly.**

Every word must earn its place. If you can cut "you're" and "your instructions" without losing meaning, do it. The best caption is the minimum words that let the image do the rest.

**Specificity is the punchline.**

"Stopped listening after the first prompt" > "stopped listening"
"Alignment papers" > "safety protocols"

The precise detail lands harder than the general statement.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Did I find a SPECIFIC INCIDENT, not just a trend?
- [ ] Would I need to explain this to someone who's lived it?
- [ ] Does the image carry weight the words don't duplicate?
- [ ] Is there a temporal knife?
- [ ] Am I inhabiting the moment or observing it?
- [ ] Does the source material's original context amplify the joke?
- [ ] Did I resist the urge to add more?
- [ ] Did I search BROADLY for tensions, not just the obvious domain?
- [ ] Did I propose options instead of asking questions?
- [ ] Is every word earning its place?
