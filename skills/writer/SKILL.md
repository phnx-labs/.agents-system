# Writer

Three modes, one voice: product copy, essays, and social posts. Detect the mode from the task and apply the right techniques. When in doubt, ask.

## Publication-First Protocol

**When writing for a specific publication (magazine, blog, newsletter), ALWAYS research before drafting.**

This is non-negotiable. Generic "good writing" gets rejected. Publications have DNA — structural patterns, argument styles, evidence standards, word limits, governance prescriptions. You must reverse-engineer that DNA before writing a single word.

### The Invariants (Non-Negotiable)

When writing for a specific publication, you MUST do all of the following. No shortcuts.

**1. Read actual published pieces (minimum 3)**

Not the submission guidelines. Not summaries. The actual articles, from different authors if possible. Guidelines tell you table stakes. Published pieces tell you what actually gets accepted.

**2. Build YOUR OWN criteria checklist for THIS publication**

As you read, extract criteria. Ask yourself for each piece:
- How did they open? What hook pattern?
- How did they structure the argument?
- What evidence types did they use? How many per claim?
- Did they address counterarguments? How?
- Did they end with prescription or just diagnosis?
- What's the word count?
- What register? (academic, conversational, polemic)
- What's conspicuously absent? (jargon, dated numbers, hedging)

Write down each criterion you discover. This checklist is unique to this publication — don't reuse a generic one.

**3. Show the checklist to the user before assessing**

Present the criteria you extracted with evidence from the pieces you read. The user should see your homework. Format:

```
CRITERIA FOR [PUBLICATION] (extracted from [Author 1], [Author 2], [Author 3]):

[ ] [Criterion 1] — Evidence: "[quote from published piece showing this]"
[ ] [Criterion 2] — Evidence: "[quote]"
[ ] [Criterion 3] — Evidence: "[quote]"
...
```

**4. Map the draft against YOUR checklist**

Go criterion by criterion. For each one, quote the specific passage from the draft that passes or fails. If it fails, say what's missing.

```
DRAFT ASSESSMENT:

[x] [Criterion 1] — PASS: "[quote from draft]"
[ ] [Criterion 2] — FAIL: Draft does X, but publication requires Y. Missing: [specific gap]
[x] [Criterion 3] — PASS: "[quote from draft]"
...
```

**5. Prioritize fixes by impact**

Structural issues first (missing sections, wrong architecture, over word limit). Sentence-level last. If the structure is wrong, perfect sentences won't save it.

```
PRIORITY FIXES:

1. [Structural] Cut X section (~600 words) — publication pieces don't do this
2. [Structural] Add governance prescription — every exemplar ended with one
3. [Evidence] Add second example for claim Y — publication uses trilogy pattern
4. [Sentence] Reduce jargon in paragraph Z — publication writes for generalists
```

### Why This Works

- Forces you to actually read, not skim
- Creates accountability — user sees your criteria, can challenge them
- Prevents generic feedback — every critique tied to a specific discovered criterion
- Prioritizes what matters — architecture before polish

## Probe Before Draft

**Don't draft first. Understand first.**

When asked to write anything:

1. **Ask WHY** — What happened? What frustrated them? What did they learn? What's the real story?
2. **Get the lived experience** — The best writing comes from specifics: names, dates, numbers, consequences. Not abstract observations.
3. **Identify the target** — Is this for a specific publication? If yes, trigger the Publication-First Protocol above.
4. **Suggest audio input** — Tell them to talk through ideas verbally. Easier than typing, captures authentic voice.
5. **THEN draft** — Only after you understand the real story and the target.

**Anti-pattern:** Guessing angles and writing 10 drafts that don't land because you never asked what the user actually experienced or where they're submitting.

## The Mindset

**You are not describing things. You are changing how the reader sees.**

- Product copy: tell people what their life becomes, not what the product does
- Essays: make the reader feel smarter, not just more informed -- give them a lens, not facts
- Social posts: share hard-won insights with peers, not "content" with an "audience"

**Universal principles across all modes:**

- Concrete before abstract. Always. The reader understands through examples and feels through specifics.
- Every sentence earns its place. If you can delete it without loss, delete it.
- Conviction over hedging. State claims earned through rigor. Hedge only for genuine uncertainty, never social anxiety.
- Simplicity over complexity. Every unnecessary word dilutes authority. The most powerful writing reads like it was carved, not typed.

## Anti-Patterns (Kill These in Any Mode)

- **Hedging out of anxiety.** "We think," "we believe," "arguably," "it seems," "perhaps." These apologize for your own claims. Delete unless genuinely uncertain.
- **Abstract before concrete.** Never introduce a concept without a tangible example within two sentences.
- **Buzzword soup.** "Leverage," "synergy," "cutting-edge," "next-generation," "dive in." Words so overused they mean nothing.
- **Content-writer transitions.** "Here's where it gets interesting." "But wait, there's more." "Let's unpack this." If the material is interesting, it will demonstrate that without announcement.
- **Summarizing closings.** "In conclusion, we have seen that..." The reader was there. Close with something new -- a question, a provocation, a reframe.
- **Throat-clearing openings.** "In recent years..." "It is well known that..." "Throughout history..." Start with the thing that matters.
- **Generic superlatives without proof.** "Best in class," "industry-leading," "world-class." Replace with a specific, verifiable claim or delete.

# Mode: Product Copy

For product launches, landing pages, taglines, marketing emails, pricing pages, feature announcements, app store descriptions, hero sections.

**Primary source:** Steve Jobs keynote analysis. See `references/steve-jobs-literary-analysis.pdf`.

## Core Principle

Values over features. "1,000 songs in your pocket" -- not "5GB storage." The spec is for the engineer. The value is for the human.

## Structural Techniques

### Three-Act Narrative
Problem (villain) -> Confrontation (existing solutions fail) -> Resolution (your product transforms everything). The problem must feel personal. The confrontation must feel familiar. The resolution must feel inevitable.

### The Villain Setup
Name the enemy. IBM. Complexity. The old way. A product without a villain is a product without a story. The villain gives your product moral weight -- it's not just better, it's right.

### Antithesis (Before/After)
Every product exists in a before/after universe. Make both vivid. "Before iPhone, getting email on your phone meant a tiny keyboard and a stylus. After iPhone, you just touched the screen."

### The Pricing Anchor
Set the expected price high. Let it sit. Then smash through with the real price. The gap IS the value proposition. "$999 would be a fair price. We're pricing it at $499."

### "One More Thing"
After the audience thinks you're done, deliver the most exciting reveal. Violated expectations amplify impact.

## Sentence-Level Techniques

### Tricolon (Rule of Three)
"An iPod. A phone. An internet communicator." Three creates rhythm, facilitates memory, implies completeness. Two is incomplete. Four is a list. Three is truth.

### Devastating Comparison
Never abstract. "5GB" means nothing. "1,000 songs in your pocket" means everything. Translate capacity into lived experience.

### Present Tense for Future
"You have six voicemails." Not "you will have." Project the audience into ownership before purchase.

### Anaphora
Repetition at the start of successive clauses builds momentum. "Don't be trapped by dogma. Don't let the noise of others' opinions drown out your inner voice."

### Compression
Every product needs one sentence that captures its essence. "The world's thinnest notebook." Not a description -- a compression of value. If you can't compress it, you don't understand it.

### The "So What?" Ladder
For every feature, chain "so what?" until you reach a human consequence. "8-core processor" -> "Renders video 4x faster" -> "You finish your edit before lunch." That last answer is your copy.

### Open Loops
Create curiosity gaps. "We've been working on something. And I think you're going to love it." The gap between what they know and what they want to know is the engine of attention.

## Format Guide

| Format | Key Rule |
|--------|----------|
| **Taglines** | One sentence. Human terms. Value compressed to minimum viable claim. "Think different." |
| **Landing page hero** | Bold claim + one visual proof. Three supporting points max. |
| **Tweets / social** | Lead with the transformation, not the feature. Numbers or contrast in first line. |
| **Feature announcements** | Villain (old way) -> Hero (your feature) -> Proof (demo or number). |
| **Pricing pages** | Anchor high, reveal low, reinforce value. Let the math persuade. |

# Mode: Essays & Long-Form

For blog posts, long-form essays, research writeups, opinion pieces, newsletters, thought leadership.

**Primary sources:** Jukic's essay craft + Gwern's analytical writing. See `references/jukic-literary-techniques.pdf` and `references/gwern-literary-techniques.pdf`.

## Core Principle

The reader finishes not just informed but equipped. They gain a lens for seeing similar situations everywhere -- a framework, not just data. (Jukic)

Write for a reader 20 years from now who lacks your context. Be explicit about assumptions, precise about claims. (Gwern)

## Opening Techniques

### The Anomaly Hook (Gwern)
Point out something that doesn't fit. "Venice ruled half the Mediterranean. And yet... it had no farms." The anomaly creates a gap the reader must resolve.

### The "Did You Know" Opener (Jukic)
Open with a superlative or counterintuitive fact. No warm-up. "The world's most industrially productive society is South Korea." Surprise is the engine.

### The Personal-Anecdotal Opener (Jukic)
Short, emotionally stakes-setting personal moment that quickly transitions to the larger argument. Never more than a paragraph. Never indulgent.

**The principle:** The opening must turn the reader into a detective. People finish essays they've been made curious about. They abandon essays that give information they didn't need.

## Structural Techniques

### Concrete-to-Cosmic Arc (Jukic)
Begin tangible -- a statistic, a scene, a corporate decision. Then progressively expand scope until you reach a civilizational question. Each transition small enough to seem natural. Twenty small steps = a journey of astonishing scope.

### Trilogy of Examples (Jukic)
When asserting a pattern, provide exactly three parallel examples from different domains. Two is coincidence. Three is a law of nature. Four is tedious.

### Evidence Cascades (Gwern)
Layer evidence types: personal observation, then statistical analysis, then literature review, then expert citation, then predictive bet. Five independent lenses make the conclusion feel overwhelming even if each alone is merely suggestive.

### The Novelty Budget (Gwern)
If your ideas are weird, make your presentation conventional. If ideas are conventional, the form can be playful. Never stack novelty on novelty.

## Persuasion Techniques

### Steelman, Then Dismantle (Jukic)
Present the strongest version of the counterargument, then methodically dismantle it. The reader who was thinking "but what about...?" finds their objection already incorporated and resolved.

### Reductio ad Absurdum with Math (Jukic)
Take a common belief. Accept it at face value. Run the actual numbers. The belief collapses under its own arithmetic. Self-verifiable destruction.

### Declarative Confidence (Gwern)
State claims flatly when earned. "The scaling hypothesis was right." Authority earned through rigor needs no softening.

### Historical Parallels (Jukic)
Deploy history not as decoration but as reframing. If the crisis has precedents, it becomes comprehensible. If comprehensible, solvable.

### Quotations as Weapons (Jukic)
Each quote must say something less credible coming from the author directly. A politician admitting failure, a scientist expressing doubt. Never use a quote that merely restates your point.

## Sentence-Level Craft

### The Punchline Sentence (Jukic)
Build context through calm exposition, then deliver a devastating understated fact. Deadpan. The reader supplies the emotion. "...with the notable exception of human beings, of which South Korea currently manages to produce only about 0.75 per woman."

### Long Then Short (Jukic)
A complex, qualifying, multi-clause sentence that builds intellectual complexity -- followed by a short sentence that lands like a hammer. "The long sentence does the thinking. The short sentence delivers the verdict."

### Parenthetical Honesty (Jukic)
Mid-sentence qualifications and em-dash interruptions create a voice thinking in real time. Trust through visible honesty.

### Mathematical Precision (Gwern)
Calibrated probability language, ISO dates, inflation-adjusted numbers. Unusual precision builds cumulative trust.

### Dry Wit (Jukic)
Humor through understatement and tonal contrast. "Overall, it looks like we sunk our industrial surplus into fake jobs." Never the point. Always earned.

## Closing Techniques

### The Rhetorical Question (Jukic)
Leave the reader with an open inquiry they cannot dismiss. The essay ends, but the thinking doesn't.

### The Metaphor Callback (Jukic)
Return to a metaphor introduced early that has been accumulating meaning. The essay closes on itself like a chord resolving.

### The Paradox Close (Gwern)
End with a truth that inverts expectation while being defensible. Forces continued thinking after the essay is done.

## Additional Literary Elements

- **Defamiliarization (Ostranenie):** Make the familiar strange. Describe what everyone knows as if seeing it for the first time. Forces actual thinking.
- **Enthymeme:** The unstated premise the reader fills in themselves. More powerful because the reader's own conclusion feels self-generated. Jukic's punchline sentences do this -- he states the fact, the reader supplies the outrage.
- **Kairos (Timeliness):** Not just what, but why NOW. The best essays feel urgent because they're timed to a moment the reader is already thinking about.
- **Ladder of Abstraction:** Move between concrete and abstract within the same paragraph. "The factory closed on Tuesday. Twelve hundred jobs. This is what economists mean by structural adjustment -- except economists rarely mention Tuesdays."
- **Chiasmus:** Reversed parallel structure. "Ask not what your country can do for you..." Once per essay max.

## Format Guide

| Format | Length | Key Pattern |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Blog posts** | 1,500-3,000 words | Full concrete-to-cosmic arc. Trilogy of examples for key claims. |
| **Long-form essays** | 5,000-10,000 words | Multiple evidence cascades. Steelman major objections. Metaphor callback close. |
| **Newsletters** | 800-1,500 words | Personal opener (1 paragraph max) -> investigation -> provocation close. |

# Mode: Social Posts

For LinkedIn posts, X/Twitter posts, threads, short-form social content.

**Primary source:** Analysis of 3,289 posts from top LinkedIn creators. See `datasets/` for raw post JSON from Deedy Das, Chip Huyen, Gergely Orosz, Nicolas Cole, Vaibhav Srivastav.

## Core Principle

You're not "creating content." You're sharing hard-won insights with peers. You post because you uncovered something others should know, have data that challenges the narrative, or experienced something that taught you. Never because you need to "stay visible."

## Opening Patterns (From Top 30 Posts)

### Data Bomb (8/30 top posts)
Lead with a specific, surprising number. No preamble.
> "Every single one of the 11 Meta superintelligence hires is an immigrant who did their undergrad abroad." -- Deedy Das (4,383 score)

### Breaking News Frame (6/30)
Position yourself as first reporter. Creates urgency.
> "Whoa: we're in the middle of what is likely the largest global software-inflicted outage" -- Gergely Orosz (11,651 score)

### Mystery Opening (5/30)
State a fact that creates a question the reader must resolve.
> "Software engineer Anna Boyko has an impressive profile... So how come no one at Coinbase knew anything of her?" -- Gergely Orosz (10,000 score)

### Personal Milestone (6/30)
Raw, unfiltered emotion. No hedging.
> "OMG I'M SO HAPPY!!! 'Designing Machine Learning Systems' is finally out!" -- Chip Huyen (21,412 score, highest in dataset)

### Provocative Claim (5/30)
Say something people will argue about. The post body is the evidence.
> "New junior developers can't actually code." -- Deedy Das (3,562 score)

## Techniques

- **Start with a real story, not a concept.** Names, dates, numbers, consequences. Not "Every OS manages process lifecycle."
- **The Reframe.** "Most people think X, but actually Y." The reframe IS the value.
- **Hero/Villain Narrative.** Name the villain (Path uploading contacts), name the hero (iOS permissions), present the danger (AI agents with unlimited access).
- **Concrete numbers over abstract claims.** "600 million contact records" not "harvested user data."
- **Short sentences for impact.** "No prompt. No consent." "You decide. Revoke anytime."
- **End with provocation, not summary.** "We solved this for apps after a scandal. Maybe we can solve it for agents before one does."

## High-Performance Patterns

| Pattern | What It Does | Example Score |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **Investigative thread** | Position as journalist uncovering something | 10,000 (842 reposts) |
| **The Lived Cost** | Exact personal price: dollars, hours | 9,211 |
| **Unfiltered Enthusiasm** | All-caps, raw emotion, no corporate polish | 21,412 (highest ever) |
| **Epistemic Signal** | "I spent the last month investigating..." | 7,889 |

## Thread Structure
First post = hook (anomaly or bold claim). Middle posts = evidence cascade compressed. Final post = provocation or reframe. Each post must stand alone AND build.

## Scheduling

| Day | Time (recipient TZ) | Open Rate |
|-----|---------------------|-----------|
| Thursday | 9-11 AM | 44% (highest) |
| Tuesday | 8-11 AM | 38% |
| Wednesday | 1-3 PM | 35% |

Avoid Mondays (inbox overload), Fridays (disengagement), weekends.

# Voice Checklist (All Modes)

Before publishing, verify:

- [ ] Does the opening create curiosity, surprise, or desire? (not inform)
- [ ] Can every sentence justify its existence? (delete any that merely restate)
- [ ] Is every abstract claim followed by a concrete example?
- [ ] Zero hedging words unless genuinely uncertain?
- [ ] Would I say this to a smart friend, not write it for a "target audience"?
- [ ] Does the closing introduce something new? (question, reframe, provocation)
- [ ] Read it aloud -- does it have rhythm?

**Product copy bonus:**
- [ ] Does every feature pass the "So What?" test?
- [ ] Is there a clear villain?
- [ ] Can the core value be stated in one sentence?

**Essay bonus:**
- [ ] Do key claims have 2+ independent evidence types?
- [ ] Are counterarguments steelmanned?
- [ ] Is the essay timed to a moment? (kairos)

**Social bonus:**
- [ ] Is there a real story with names, dates, or numbers?
- [ ] Does the reader learn something they didn't know?
- [ ] Did I show my homework?
