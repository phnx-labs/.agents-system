# Writing -- How to Write a Blog Post

This is not a template. Templates produce template-shaped writing. This is a way of thinking about what makes a post worth reading.

## Before You Write

### Find the Idea, Not the Topic

This mirrors how `/image-craft` extracts ideas from content for visual work. The same principle applies to writing.

A **topic** is what the post is "about" -- AI agents, pricing strategy, remote work. Topics are categories. They're what you'd tag the post with. Nobody gets excited about a category.

An **idea** is what makes THIS post different from every other post filed under the same topic. It's the insight, the tension, the thing the reader didn't know they didn't know.

| Topic | Idea |
|-------|------|
| AI agents | The bottleneck isn't the models -- it's that we're still designing interfaces like it's 2023 |
| Pricing | Your free tier is training users to expect your product for free |
| Remote work | The office was never about productivity -- it was about performance |

If you can state your idea in one sentence that would make someone argue with you or forward it to a friend, you have something worth writing. If your one-sentence summary is "here's what you need to know about X," you have a Wikipedia article, not a blog post.

### Research Before You Draft

Read the `/writer` skill for the full protocol. The key points:

- If writing for a specific audience or publication, study what they actually read and share -- not what you think they should care about
- Find real data, real quotes, real examples. The reader can smell when you're hand-waving.
- Check what already exists on this topic. If 50 posts say the same thing, either find a genuinely new angle or don't write the post.

## Structure

### Openings

The opening has one job: make the reader need to know what comes next.

**The anomaly:** Point out something that doesn't fit. "The cost of intelligence collapsed 280x in 18 months. Yet most people are still chatting." The gap between those two facts is the engine.

**The provocation:** State something the reader will resist. "Your AI strategy is already obsolete." Now they have to find out why.

**The scene:** Put the reader somewhere specific. "Boris Cherny landed 259 pull requests in 30 days. Every single line was written by Claude. He doesn't write code anymore." The reader is now watching over Boris's shoulder.

**What never works:** "In recent years, the field of artificial intelligence has seen remarkable advances..." This is throat-clearing. The reader was already bored by "in recent years."

### Middles

The middle is where most posts fail. The opening hooks, the ending resolves, but the middle is where the reader decides to stay or leave.

**Every section must earn its position.** If you can rearrange sections without the reader noticing, the structure has no logic. Each section should follow from the previous one -- building, complicating, or turning.

**Concrete before abstract.** Start with the example, the story, the data point. THEN draw the conclusion. Not the other way around. "Here's the principle, and here's an example" puts the reader to sleep. "Here's what happened, and here's what it means" pulls them forward.

**Vary the texture.** Dense argument, then a short anecdote. Data, then a human moment. Technical detail, then a step back to the big picture. Monotone -- whether it's all storytelling or all analysis -- is numbing.

### Endings

**Don't summarize.** The reader was there. "In conclusion, we have seen that AI agents are transforming..." is an insult to their attention.

Instead:
- **Reframe:** Give the reader a new way to see what they just read. Zoom out. Connect it to something larger.
- **Provoke:** End with a question the reader will keep thinking about after they close the tab.
- **Call forward:** Point to what comes next -- not your next blog post, but what happens next in the world you just described.

## Voice

### Finding the Blog's Voice

Every blog has a voice. Before writing, read 3-5 existing posts and absorb it. Ask:

- **Register:** Is it formal or conversational? Academic or street-level?
- **Stance:** Does it take positions or present options? Argue or inform?
- **Density:** Short punchy sentences or long flowing ones? Sparse or detailed?
- **Audience assumption:** Does it explain terms or assume knowledge?

If no existing posts exist, establish a voice that fits the subject matter and audience. A startup's engineering blog sounds different from a wellness brand's journal. Match the voice to who's reading and why.

### What to Avoid

Read the `/writer` skill's anti-patterns section. The key ones:
- Hedging out of anxiety ("we believe," "arguably," "perhaps")
- Abstract before concrete
- Buzzword soup ("leverage," "synergy," "cutting-edge")
- Content-writer transitions ("Here's where it gets interesting")
- Throat-clearing openings ("In recent years...")

## Length

No fixed rule. Length follows content:
- A provocative argument can land in 800 words
- A technical deep-dive might need 3000
- Most posts land between 1200-2000

The test: if you can cut 200 words without losing anything, the post is 200 words too long. If you need 200 more words to make a section click, it's 200 words too short.

## SEO Without Selling Your Soul

SEO matters but it should never dictate the writing. The order of priority:

1. Write something genuinely worth reading
2. Make the title clear and specific (search engines and humans both prefer this)
3. Write a description that makes someone click in search results
4. Use the target keyword naturally -- if you have to force it, the post isn't really about that keyword
5. Link to related posts on the site (good for readers AND SEO)

Never write for search engines at the expense of readers. A post that ranks but nobody finishes is worse than a post that doesn't rank but gets forwarded.
